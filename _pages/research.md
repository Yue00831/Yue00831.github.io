My dissertation investigates how **animacy** — the conceptual distinction between living and non-living entities — shapes syntactic choices in human language production and in large language models. I approach this question through three interconnected studies.

---

## Dissertation Studies

### Study 1 — Behavioral Cross-Linguistic Study
A production experiment examining animacy effects on object relative clause (ORC) production across speakers of L1 English, L1 Spanish, and L1 Mandarin. Participants complete sentence fragments that manipulate the animacy of head nouns and agent nouns, allowing direct comparison of voice selection strategies across typologically diverse languages.

### Study 2 — Representational Analysis (GPT-2)
A decoding analysis of GPT-2 internal representations showing **92% animacy decoding accuracy**, alongside a representation-behavior dissociation: the model encodes animacy robustly yet does not always deploy it in the same way human speakers do.

### Study 3 — Mechanistic Interpretability
Using **layerwise activation patching** and **attention head ablation** in GPT-2, I identify 18 critical attention heads (including L9H3 and L2H0) involved in voice selection. A key finding: **agent noun animacy — not head noun animacy — drives voice preference**, with head noun patching effects peaking causally in early layers and agent noun patching peaking in late layers.

## Other Projects

### Similarity-Based Interference in L2 ORC Production
With [Elaine J. Francis](https://cla.purdue.edu/english/francislab/) (Purdue). Investigates whether similarity-based interference (SBI) occurs in second-language production of English object relative clauses among L1-Mandarin speakers, examining structural parallels to reading-time interference effects.

### Quantifier Scope and LLMs
With Yue Fang & Yan Cong. A corpus and behavioral study of quantifier scope disambiguation, presented at LREC 2026 and HSP 2026.
